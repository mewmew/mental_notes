Sensationalistic LSD Findings In Perspective

Despite recent news coverage, the first acid brain scans fall short of explaining the most profound aspects of tripping

Last week, the scientific journal Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences published a study of the first brain scans ever done of people tripping on LSD. The research team, headed by former drug advisor to the UK government David Nutt, reported acid-induced effects on the brain that were associated with the experience of potent visual hallucinations and, most notably, a feeling of personal identity loss. In a story in The Guardian, Professor Nutt claimed that this finding “is to neuroscience what the Higgs boson was to particle physics.” This analogy shouldn’t be taken lightly — after all, the Higgs boson was the last remaining particle that needed to be discovered in order to verify The Standard Model, the theory that explains how the basic building blocks of matter interact. 
 
 Amid all the excitement it’s important to fairly assess what was (and what was not) revealed by these tripping scans. As a doctoral student in neuroscience, I have no vested interest in criticizing or applauding this scientific finding. But if we assume the neuroscientific equivalent of discovering the Higgs boson is uncovering the secret to consciousness in the brain, it’s safe to say we’re not even close to achieving this.

In the experiment, researchers used functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) to scan the brains of individuals on days when they were given intravenous injections of 75 micrograms of LSD and on days when they were given a salty placebo solution. This type of study design allows researchers to subtract the pattern of brain activity observed during a LSD high from the activity of the exact same brain that might be expecting a high after being given an uncertain substance. Ultimately, such a subtraction is essential for pinning down the effects that are exclusive to LSD — and this is where things get interesting.

They uncovered that LSD has some fascinating effects on the visual cortex, the region at the back of our heads that receives electrical signals originating from the eyes. During a trip, this area was found to receive a substantial boost in blood flow, presumably to fuel a firework display of brain activity. But more important, LSD also dramatically increased the strength with which the visual cortex communicated to over 20 distinct brain areas that it normally doesn’t interact with very much. This included regions such as Heschl’s gyrus, which typically processes auditory inputs from the ears. The extent of this “desegregation” between brain regions that are normally isolated from each other correlated with how strongly individuals rated their own visual hallucinations.

The brain scans are incapable of shedding light on acid’s more deeply psychological effects, such as ego-dissolution. This speaks to the fundamental limitations of neuroscience.

This finding is undoubtedly insightful. Since the day you were born, your brain has undergone a decades-long process of refining the connections between its billions of neurons to allow you to adapt to your environment. The connections between your eyes and the visual cortex became strengthened as its cells spent years receiving visual inputs and learning to recognize what they mean. In contrast, connections that might have existed between your visual cortex and brain regions that receive taste information from your tongue or touch information from your skin became weaker. After all, most things you see are out of reach and never make it to your mouth, so there isn’t enough of a relationship between the different senses to justify keeping the communication channels between them open. Brain development is all about establishing order, and in fact some developmental psychologists argue that all babies start off with a tangled mess of experience, in which all the senses randomly interact.

In light of this, the recent brain scans have unlocked something fundamental about the rich and random multisensory experience that is the hallmark of the LSD high — it might be underpinned by the fact that the substance enhances cross talk between regions that have become strangers to each other, perhaps throughout the course of brain development. It’s important to note, however, that this idea isn’t entirely new. Researchers working with drugs such as psilocybin mushrooms and mescaline have previously suggested that psychedelic consciousness might be brought about by the brain entering a state of increased disorder known as the “the entropic brain.”

But acid trips aren’t just about altered sensation. When describing their experiences with the drug, many people feel that the most profoundly life-changing effects of LSD stem from the fact that it can strip individuals of their sense of self, while giving them a radical sense of unity with the world. Unfortunately, the brain scans are incapable of shedding light on acid’s more deeply psychological effects, such as ego-dissolution. This isn’t a flaw in the study. Rather, it speaks to the fundamental limitations of neuroscience, for now.

In this experiment, the researchers found that during an LSD high participants’ experiences of ego-dissolution and the sense that things took on an altered meaning were associated with weaker cross talk within a particular network that involves brain regions such as the parahippocampal gyrus and retrosplenial cortex. Now, you probably feel that this sentence has told you nothing about the origins of ego-dissolution in the brain. The problem is, it means just about as little to a neuroscientist as it does to you. Neuroscientists know where these regions are located — they are shown below, so now you know almost as much as I do about them — but that is roughly where it ends.

What do we know? To take the parahippocampal gyrus as an example, we know that it becomes activated when people look at images of scenes (i.e., landscapes or buildings). Damage to this area is known to impair a person’s ability to remember episodes from their life and to find their way around new spaces. It can also diminish a person’s ability to comprehend sarcasm and the tendency to feel displeasure when hearing dissonant music that most people find aversive. That’s a pretty diverse range of effects, don’t you agree? When it comes to the second brain region mentioned, the retrosplenial cortex, neuroscientists have put forth several decent hypotheses regarding its function but have yet to turn all the scattered pieces of evidence into a coherent theory.

The essence of the problem is that neuroscience is so new the knowledge we have about the functions of specific brain regions are merely droplets in the ocean that makes up the brain. This means that when a scientific report tells you that a particular network of brain regions becomes silent under the influence of LSD, it couldn’t possibly explain something as complex as why acid makes people lose their sense of self and experience a profoundly altered sense of consciousness.

In saying “We didn’t know how [the] profound effects [of LSD] were produced — it was too difficult to do,” the principal investigator behind the recent study implied that our lack of knowledge is in the past. But as things stand, I would say that it’s still the status quo. The recent brain scans shed some light on the perceptual elements of the LSD high, but they couldn’t scrape the surface of the most sacred mind-altering aspects of the experience. Ego-dissolution, spirituality, and oneness with the universe remain inaccessible and veiled in mystery, even when we are peering directly into the brain. And so, dare I say, the Higgs boson of neuroscience has still not been found.
